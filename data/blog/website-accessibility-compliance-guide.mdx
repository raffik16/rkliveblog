In 2023, over 4,500 website accessibility lawsuits were filed in the United States alone. The average settlement? $25,000-$50,000. For larger companies, settlements regularly exceed $100,000.

And here's the uncomfortable reality: most website owners have no idea if their site is compliant. They assume "it probably is" or "nobody has complained." That's not a legal strategy—it's wishful thinking.

I've helped remediate dozens of websites after accessibility complaints. The work is expensive, stressful, and entirely preventable. Let me explain what you actually need to know.

## The Legal Landscape (In Plain English)

### The ADA and Websites

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) was passed in 1990, before the commercial internet existed. The law requires "places of public accommodation" to be accessible to people with disabilities.

Courts have increasingly interpreted websites as places of public accommodation, particularly when they connect to physical businesses or provide essential services. This interpretation has been upheld at the federal level, though specifics vary by circuit.

**What this means practically:** If you operate a commercial website in the United States, courts can (and do) find that the ADA applies to you.

### WCAG: The Technical Standard

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) aren't law, but they're the de facto standard courts and plaintiffs reference. WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the typical target for compliance.

WCAG covers four principles (POUR):
- **Perceivable:** Users can perceive content (alt text, captions, color contrast)
- **Operable:** Users can navigate and interact (keyboard navigation, no time limits)
- **Understandable:** Content and interfaces are clear (readable text, predictable behavior)
- **Robust:** Content works with assistive technologies (semantic HTML, ARIA)

### Who Gets Sued?

Some businesses face higher risk:
- **E-commerce sites** (lawsuit favorites—clear commercial nexus)
- **Hospitality and food service** (hotels, restaurants with online booking)
- **Healthcare** (online portals, appointment scheduling)
- **Financial services** (banking, loan applications)
- **Education** (enrollment, course materials)

But any commercial website can be targeted. I've seen lawsuits against small local businesses with minimal web presence.

### The Lawsuit Pattern

Most accessibility lawsuits follow a template:

1. Law firm sends demand letter citing specific violations
2. Violations are typically legitimate (they test before sending)
3. Letter demands settlement ($10K-$50K typically) plus remediation
4. Most businesses settle—fighting costs more than settling
5. Repeat plaintiffs file hundreds of similar cases

This isn't necessarily bad-faith litigation. The websites actually are inaccessible. But the enforcement-by-lawsuit model creates a particular dynamic.

## Common Accessibility Violations

After auditing hundreds of websites, certain issues appear repeatedly:

### 1. Missing or Inadequate Alt Text

Images without alt text are invisible to screen readers. Users who are blind have no idea what the image contains.

**Bad:**
```html
<img src="product-photo.jpg">
```

**Good:**
```html
<img src="product-photo.jpg" alt="Blue running shoes, side view showing cushioned sole">
```

**Also bad:**
```html
<img src="product-photo.jpg" alt="image" >
<!-- or -->
<img src="product-photo.jpg" alt="product-photo.jpg">
```

Alt text should describe the content meaningfully, not just acknowledge the image exists.

### 2. Poor Color Contrast

Text needs sufficient contrast against its background. WCAG requires 4.5:1 contrast ratio for normal text, 3:1 for large text.

Light gray text on white backgrounds fails. That trendy low-contrast design? It's inaccessible.

**Tools to check:**
- WebAIM Contrast Checker
- Chrome DevTools (inspect element → color picker shows contrast ratio)

### 3. Keyboard Navigation Failures

Many users can't use a mouse. Everything on your site should be accessible via keyboard:
- Tab moves between interactive elements
- Enter/Space activates buttons and links
- Escape closes modals
- Arrow keys navigate within components

Common failures:
- Custom dropdown menus that don't respond to keyboard
- Modal dialogs that can't be closed with Escape
- Focus getting "trapped" in components
- Skip links missing (users must Tab through entire navigation)

### 4. Missing Form Labels

Screen readers announce form labels to users. Without proper labels, users don't know what information a field requires.

**Bad:**
```html
<input type="text" placeholder="Email address">
```

**Good:**
```html
<label for="email">Email address</label>
<input type="text" id="email" name="email">
```

Placeholders are not labels—they disappear when users start typing.

### 5. Videos Without Captions

Video content needs captions for users who are deaf or hard of hearing. Auto-generated captions are better than nothing, but professional captions are the standard.

### 6. PDFs and Documents

PDFs are frequently inaccessible. Scanned documents without OCR, PDFs without tagged structure, complex tables—all create barriers.

### 7. Ambiguous Link Text

Screen reader users often navigate by links. "Click here" and "Read more" don't explain where the link goes.

**Bad:**
```html
To learn about our services, <a href="/services">click here</a>.
```

**Good:**
```html
Learn more about <a href="/services">our consulting services</a>.
```

## Approaches to Remediation

### Option 1: Full Manual Remediation

This means auditing every page and fixing every issue. It's thorough but expensive.

**Typical cost:** $5,000-$50,000+ depending on site size and complexity

**Timeline:** Weeks to months

**Pros:**
- Most thorough approach
- Addresses root causes
- Best long-term solution

**Cons:**
- High upfront cost
- Requires specialized expertise
- Needs ongoing maintenance as site changes

### Option 2: Accessibility Widgets/Overlays

Accessibility widgets add a layer on top of your website that provides additional accessibility features: text resizing, contrast adjustments, screen reader optimization, and more.

**Examples:** UserWay, accessiBe, AudioEye, EqualWeb

**Typical cost:** $50-$500/month

**Timeline:** Implementation in hours

**Pros:**
- Fast implementation
- Lower initial cost
- Continuous updates
- Some level of protection (though not complete)

**Cons:**
- Doesn't fix underlying issues
- Can conflict with native assistive technologies
- Some accessibility advocates criticize overlays
- Not a complete legal shield

### Option 3: Hybrid Approach

Fix critical issues manually while using a widget for enhanced features. This is often the most practical balance.

**My recommendation:** Use this approach. Fix the major issues (alt text, form labels, keyboard navigation basics), then add a widget for additional customization options.

### What Widgets Can and Can't Do

**Widgets CAN:**
- Provide users immediate customization options
- Fix some issues automatically (ARIA attributes, focus indicators)
- Offer ongoing monitoring and alerts
- Show good faith effort toward compliance
- Help with simpler issues at scale

**Widgets CAN'T:**
- Fix fundamental structural issues
- Replace proper semantic HTML
- Guarantee complete WCAG compliance
- Provide absolute legal protection
- Replace human accessibility expertise for complex sites

### The Overlay Controversy

Some accessibility advocates strongly criticize overlay solutions. Their arguments:

1. Overlays can interfere with native screen reader behavior
2. They address symptoms, not causes
3. Some make false compliance claims
4. Users with disabilities report negative experiences with some products

These criticisms have merit. Overlays are not a magic solution.

However, the alternative—doing nothing because full remediation is expensive—is worse. For many small businesses, a widget combined with basic manual fixes is a practical starting point.

**My position:** Widgets are a tool, not a complete solution. Use them as part of a broader accessibility strategy, not as a replacement for one.

## Creating an Accessibility Strategy

Here's the practical approach I recommend:

### Step 1: Audit Your Current State

Before fixing anything, understand what's broken.

**Free automated tools:**
- WAVE (wave.webaim.org)
- axe DevTools (browser extension)
- Lighthouse accessibility audit (Chrome DevTools)

**These will find:**
- Missing alt text
- Color contrast issues
- Missing form labels
- Empty buttons/links
- ARIA problems

**They won't find:**
- Keyboard navigation issues
- Focus order problems
- Complex interaction failures
- Content clarity issues

For a complete picture, combine automated testing with manual testing (actually using keyboard navigation, testing with screen readers).

### Step 2: Fix High-Impact Issues

Not all issues are equal. Prioritize:

**Critical (fix immediately):**
- Images without any alt text
- Forms without labels
- Buttons/links with no accessible name
- Color contrast below 3:1

**High (fix soon):**
- Color contrast below 4.5:1
- Keyboard navigation failures
- Missing skip links
- Videos without captions

**Medium (address during development):**
- Suboptimal alt text
- Heading hierarchy issues
- Focus styling improvements

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are accessibility widgets actually effective?

Widgets provide real value—user customization, automated fixes for some issues, and ongoing monitoring. They're not perfect, but they're better than nothing. Use them as part of a broader strategy.

### Do I need WCAG 2.1 or 2.2 compliance?

WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the current practical standard. WCAG 2.2 was released in 2023 and adds additional criteria, but most enforcement still references 2.1. Aim for 2.1 AA and address 2.2 requirements as feasible.

### What if I receive a demand letter?

Don't panic. Don't ignore it. Consult an attorney experienced in ADA/accessibility matters. Many of these matters settle for less than demanded, especially with documented remediation efforts.

### How long does remediation take?

Basic fixes (alt text, labels, contrast) can be done in days. Full remediation of a complex site takes weeks to months. Widgets can be implemented in hours.

### Does my industry affect risk?

Yes. E-commerce, hospitality, healthcare, financial services, and education face more lawsuits. But any commercial site can be targeted.

### Are WordPress/Shopify sites automatically accessible?

No. The platforms provide accessible-capable infrastructure, but theme choices, content, and plugins often introduce issues. You still need to audit and address problems.

### Can I just add "screen reader only" text everywhere?

That's a band-aid, not a solution. Proper semantic HTML and clear structure are more sustainable and effective than hiding text for screen readers.
